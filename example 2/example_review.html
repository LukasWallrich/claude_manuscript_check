OVERALL SUMMARY

This accepted manuscript presents two well-structured studies examining valuing diversity as a mediator of intergroup contact effects. The writing is generally clear and professional, with a logical structure and appropriate use of APA conventions. The main issues identified include inconsistent OSF data availability links across pages (with different view_only tokens), duplicate keywords on the title page, heading capitalization inconsistencies, a spelling error, and the use of 'marginally significant' language for a non-significant result. The manuscript includes appropriate compliance statements and commendable open science practices.

1 issue: 1 Major

The manuscript presents a well-designed pair of complementary studies. Study 1 uses a UK student sample with multiple outcome measures, while Study 2 uses a representative German population sample with behavioural intention measures and additional control variables. The limitations section is thorough and transparent. Metacheck flagged 8 instances of potential causal language in the abstract, but after evaluation, all are standard mediation terminology ('predict,' 'mediates,' 'offer a lever') rather than genuine causal overclaims — the authors explicitly acknowledge in the Limitations section that cross-sectional analyses cannot establish causality. Marginal significance language
Location: Results, Study 1 (p. 9) Major
Original: that difference was largest and marginally significant in the case of affective prejudice
Suggestion: that difference was largest, though not statistically significant, in the case of affective prejudice

Explanation: The use of 'marginally significant' language is discouraged in contemporary quantitative psychology. The confidence interval reported (Δr = .20, 95% CI [.00, .40]) includes zero at its lower bound, indicating the difference is not statistically significant at the .05 level. Consider describing the result as non-significant while noting the effect size and confidence interval. This is consistent with the dagger (†) significance marker issue noted in Category 6 (Tables).

Observations

Introduction and background: The introduction effectively synthesises literature from diversity beliefs, intergroup contact theory, and mediation research. The progression from establishing the importance of valuing diversity, through the contact hypothesis, to the gap in research linking these two literatures is clear and compelling. The rationale for the specific hypotheses is well-grounded in theory.
Limitations discussion: The limitations section is commendably thorough, addressing: (1) the focus on majority-group perspectives only, (2) the cross-sectional design limiting causal inference, (3) the use of behavioural intentions rather than actual behaviours, and (4) the bidirectional relationship between valuing diversity and contact. The authors appropriately call for experimental and longitudinal follow-up research.
Sample description: Both samples are well-described. Study 1 provides details on demographics (N = 217, 89% female, ages 18–45), recruitment across five universities with varying diversity contexts, and data collection methods. Study 2 describes the ALLBUS sampling procedure, exclusion criteria, and resulting sample characteristics (N = 2,618, 49.3% female, mean age 50.8). The gender imbalance in Study 1 is not explicitly discussed as a limitation — this could be briefly noted, though it is typical of psychology student samples.
Statistical methods and effect sizes: The analytical approaches are appropriate: SEM with full-information maximum likelihood for Study 1, and SEM with multiple imputation via mice and lavaan.survey for Study 2 (accounting for complex survey design). Bootstrapped and Monte Carlo confidence intervals for indirect effects are reported. Effect sizes are presented throughout via standardised coefficients and confidence intervals. The authors appropriately note that indirect effects in Study 2 are small and contextualise this with reference to Greenwald et al. (2015) on the societal implications of small effects.
Data and code availability: Data for Study 1 and analysis code for both studies are shared via OSF, which is commendable. Study 2 uses the ALLBUS dataset, for which data access instructions are provided. This supports reproducibility, though the inconsistent OSF links need to be resolved (see Category 10, Funding & Compliance).
