OVERALL SUMMARY

This manuscript presents two studies examining whether valuing diversity mediates the effects of intergroup contact on prejudice and behavioral intentions. The writing is generally clear and the research is well-structured. However, two critical referencing issues were identified: two cited sources are missing from the reference list entirely. Several major issues include duplicated keywords, inconsistent data availability links, heading formatting inconsistencies, and a typo. Minor issues relate to the short abstract, missing ORCID IDs, and formatting details.

12. Statistical Reporting metacheck

Minor Issue 12.1 — “Marginally significant” language
Location: Results, Study 1 (p. 9)
“that difference was largest and marginally significant in the case of affective prejudice”
Action: Consider revising to avoid “marginally significant” language. For example: “that difference was largest for affective prejudice, though the confidence interval for the difference included zero (Δr = .20, 95% CI [.00, .40]).”
The term “marginally significant” is widely discouraged in quantitative research because it implies that a non-significant result (p > .05) provides evidence for an effect, which inflates the Type I error rate. Results should be reported as significant or non-significant relative to the prespecified alpha level.
