8. Language

2 issues: 2 Major

Location: The Present Research — Hypothesis 5 (p. 251) Major
Original: H5: Thinking of Muslims and refugees would moderate (strengthen) the association between attitudes towards Muslims and refuges and attitudes towards foreigners generally.
Suggestion: H5: Thinking of Muslims and refugees would moderate (strengthen) the association between attitudes towards Muslims and refugees and attitudes towards foreigners generally.

Explanation: “refuges” (places of shelter) should be “refugees” (people seeking refuge). This typo appears in a prominent location — a numbered hypothesis — and could be confusing to readers.

Location: Results — Religion of Salient Groups (p. 256–257) Major
Original: the resulting regression models is shown in Table 4.
Suggestion: the resulting regression model is shown in Table 4.

Explanation: Subject-verb agreement error. Since a single regression model is presented in Table 4, the subject should be singular (“model”) to agree with the singular verb (“is”). Alternatively, if the plural “models” is intended, the verb should be “are shown.”

11. Light Content Checks

These are observations, not required fixes. They are intended as constructive suggestions.

Introduction & background: The introduction provides thorough background on attitudes towards foreigners, the availability heuristic, outgroup heterogeneity, and the specific German context. The literature review is well-organized into thematic subsections.
Limitations: The authors discuss limitations thoughtfully, including the cross-sectional design (which precludes causal inference), the focus on a single country, the potential for reverse causation, and the inability to test multiple dimensions of distance. This is a thorough treatment.
Sample description: The sample is well-described (N = 3,195, Mage = 51.4 years, SD = 18.2, 49.8% female, 91.5% born in Germany). The sampling procedure (ALLBUS random cluster sampling, 162 sample points, 34.6% response rate) and weighting approach are clearly reported.
Effect sizes: Unstandardized regression coefficients (B) and standard errors are reported in all tables. R² values are provided for overall models. The authors also describe effect sizes in substantive terms (e.g., “about 0.4 standard deviations more negative”). Consider also reporting standardized coefficients (β) to facilitate cross-study comparisons.
Data/code availability: Full analysis code in R and the coding framework are shared on OSF. The preregistration on AsPredicted is also referenced, with transparent discussion of deviations. This represents excellent open science practice.
Causal language: [metacheck] Metacheck detected potential causal claims in the abstract. Given the cross-sectional, observational design, the language used (“moderates the relationship,” “suggesting an attenuation of the links through familiarity”) is generally appropriately hedged. The authors clearly acknowledge the cross-sectional limitation in the Discussion. Worth double-checking that all statements in the abstract avoid implying directionality that the design cannot support.
